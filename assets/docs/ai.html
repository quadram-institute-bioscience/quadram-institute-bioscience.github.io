Responsible use of AI at the Quadram Institute

Use AI to support curiosity, clarity and careful research. Do not use it to outsource judgement, bypass understanding, expose sensitive data or hide uncertainty.

Co-pilot, not driver

Keep the human in the research loop

AI is most useful when it sharpens expert work. It becomes risky when it replaces understanding, supervision, review or judgement.

1

Use AI to learn faster

Ask for explanations, assumptions, method summaries and questions to take to a supervisor or statistician.

2

Use AI to structure ideas

Let it organise themes, headings or possible outlines, while keeping the scientific argument yours.

3

Use AI as a sounding board

Ask it to identify gaps, caveats and reviewer-style questions before human peer review.

Before you paste, prompt or publish

Run a quick risk check

Answer four questions. The result updates immediately and gives a recommended next step.

1. Does your prompt include personal, clinical, participant, confidential, unpublished or restricted information?
2. Are you asking AI to make a final scientific, clinical, ethical or legal decision?
3. Will the output be shared, submitted, published or used in a formal research workflow?
4. Can you verify, explain and defend the output without relying on the AI?
Not assessed Answer the questions to assess risk.

Use this as a pause point before using a public AI tool, especially when working with research, clinical, partner or unpublished material.

Prompt lab

Ask for help in ways that preserve responsibility

Filter examples of better and poorer prompts. Good prompts ask AI to support learning, structure and critique without inventing evidence or handling restricted data.

Better

Better

Scenario cards

What should you do?

Use these examples in lab meetings, induction, supervision or research-group discussions.

A

Clinical metadata

You want to paste a dataset with microbiome and clinical metadata into a public AI tool to get an analysis plan.

Pause. Consider identifiability, agreements, ethics approval and tool approval. Use approved institutional routes or ask a data governance lead before using AI.
B

AI-generated citations

AI suggests ten papers for your introduction, with convincing titles and authors.

Verify every reference in trusted databases. Do not cite papers unless they exist, are relevant and support the claim being made.
C

Positive feedback, poor understanding

A student gets good feedback on an AI-generated table but does not understand how it was produced.

Do not build on work that the researcher cannot explain. Use supervision to reconstruct the method, assumptions and next steps.
D

Open-source AI model

Your team wants to publish code or a model that others can reuse.

Share where possible, but document limitations, licences, intended uses and misuse risks. Consider responsible AI licensing for sensitive applications.

Research culture

Responsible AI is also about mentorship

AI can reduce friction, but it cannot replace clear feedback, training, constructive criticism or community.

What should supervisors and line managers model?
Discuss acceptable and unacceptable AI use openly. Give examples. Encourage disclosure rather than secrecy. Reinforce that understanding matters more than speed.
What should staff and students record?
Record the tool used, date, purpose, prompt summary, output used and checks performed when AI materially contributes to writing, coding, analysis or figures.
When should I ask for human advice?
Ask when data is sensitive, outputs affect research conclusions, you feel uncertain, you cannot explain the result, or AI use is increasing anxiety rather than clarity.
How should AI support open research?
Use AI to improve documentation, metadata, code comments and plain-language summaries, while protecting restricted data and applying clear licences and safeguards.

Eight principles

The Quadram standard in one screen

Use these principles as a short code of practice for everyday AI use.

1

Human responsibility

People remain accountable for research decisions, outputs and impacts.

2

Scientific rigour

AI outputs must be checked, validated and understood.

3

Data protection

Do not enter sensitive or restricted data into unapproved tools.

4

Transparency

Record and disclose meaningful AI use where relevant.

5

Fairness and respect

Do not misrepresent people or misuse creative, scientific or personal work.

6

Open with safeguards

Share research assets with licences, documentation and misuse protections.

7

Learning and mentorship

AI should support learning, not replace supervision or peer feedback.

8

Public trust

AI use should strengthen confidence in food, microbiology, gut health and disease research.

Use AI with curiosity, care and accountability.

Responsible AI at the Quadram Institute means combining innovation with rigour, transparency, data protection, good mentorship and public trust.
